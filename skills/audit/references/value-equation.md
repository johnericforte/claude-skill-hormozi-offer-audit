# Value Equation Reference Card

> Source: Alex Hormozi, _$100M Offers_ (2021). Framework cited descriptively under nominative fair use.

## The formula

```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
```

- Numerator: multiplicative.
- Denominator: multiplicative.
- To raise perceived value: increase numerator OR decrease denominator. Doing both compounds.

## The four levers

### 1. Dream Outcome

The result the prospect wants, in their language, not yours.

- "Cut my no-show rate" beats "use a client management tool"
- "Get my evenings back" beats "automate my workflow"
- "Hit $10K MRR by Q3" beats "grow my SaaS"

Audit question: does the offer name a result the buyer would say out loud, or does it name a category / feature / tool?

### 2. Perceived Likelihood of Achievement

The buyer's belief that they will get the Dream Outcome if they buy. Driven by:

- Proof: case studies, named clients, specific numbers
- Social proof: testimonials, user counts, named logos
- Authority: credentials, citations, third-party validation
- Guarantees: money-back, performance, anti-guarantee
- Specificity: "1,247 agencies" beats "thousands"

Audit question: does the offer give the buyer a reason to believe the Dream Outcome will actually happen for them?

### 3. Time Delay

The interval between purchase and outcome. Lower = better.

- Immediate value (download, instant access, day-one win) beats
- Short delay (7-day onboarding, week-1 milestone) beats
- Medium delay (30-day program) beats
- Long delay (6-month transformation, year-long course)

Audit question: when does the buyer see the first sign that this is working?

### 4. Effort & Sacrifice

What the buyer must give up or do to get the Dream Outcome. Lower = better.

- Effort: time, learning curve, decisions, setup, ongoing work
- Sacrifice: money, status, comfort, alternatives, identity shifts

Audit question: what is the buyer giving up by saying yes? Money is one piece; effort and sacrifice usually outweigh it.

## Scoring rubric (0-25 per lever)

Use this when running an audit. Document the score with one sentence of reasoning.

| Score | Meaning |
| ----- | ------- |
| 0-5   | Lever absent or actively negative. The offer ignores or harms this lever. |
| 6-10  | Lever weak. Mentioned but generic, vague, or unsupported. |
| 11-15 | Lever average. Present and specific but not a standout strength. |
| 16-20 | Lever strong. Concrete, proof-backed, ahead of category norms. |
| 21-25 | Lever exceptional. The offer wins on this lever alone. |

Total = sum of 4 lever scores. Range: 0-100.

## How to flag the weakest lever

After scoring, the weakest lever is the audit's primary fix target. The Top 3 Fixes block always includes at least one fix targeting the weakest lever.

If two levers tie for lowest, pick the one with the lower lift (cheaper to fix wins). Effort & Sacrifice is usually cheaper to fix than Perceived Likelihood (which needs proof assets).

## Common audit findings

- **Hero copy describes a category, not an outcome** → Dream Outcome score < 10. Fix: rewrite hero with named result.
- **Adjectives without proof ("powerful", "easy", "fast")** → Perceived Likelihood < 10. Fix: replace each adjective with a number, named entity, or case study.
- **Free trial with no day-by-day milestone** → Time Delay < 15. Fix: name what the buyer will see by Day X.
- **"Try free" but onboarding is 8 hours of setup** → Effort & Sacrifice < 10. Fix: add a done-for-you tier OR shorten setup.
